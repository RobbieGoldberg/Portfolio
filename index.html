Saleblazers
Multiplayer open-world shopkeeping survival game

At Airstrafe Interactive, I was a core gameplay engineer on Saleblazers leading up to and carrying through its early access release. My contributions to the game include building new large-scale systems such as achievement unlocks and text localization, as well as improving existing systems like music layer transitions, quest triggers, dialogue events, inventory flow, and networking behavior. I created new in-game items and machines with unique functionalities, like spellbook weapons with elemental projectiles and anvil stations that increase item durability.

Another large facet of my work on this team involved creating tools for use by other engineers, designers, and artists. One tool I built automatically tracked scene changes and provided easy access to re-bake navmesh, occlusion culling, and proxy data for scenes with relevant modifications. This reduced overhead and time spent by designers and engineers who were previously keeping track of changes and manually re-baking assets on a per-scene basis. One other tool I built could locate all dialogue databases and string tables in the project, export them to CSV, run them through machine translation, and re-import them for localization. This provided a simple pipeline for designers to update assets with translations after writing new dialogue or other in-game text. Another tool located and selectively modified all materials in the project so that artists could easily apply changes to many materials at once. Lastly, I wrote two systems to help designers and artists deal with in-game fog: one to re-color the game's global fog on a zone-by-zone basis and one to move fog during cutscenes so that selective locations being shown to the player would not be obscured.
Bloompunk
Botanical first-person arena shooter roguelike

Bloompunk was a capstone project made at University of Southern California by a team of 30 students over two semesters. Working on this game was the closest approximation to realistic industry work I had experienced before graduating. We had full design, art, and audio teams, as well as dedicated producers to keep us on course. Being a programmer on this project was the first time in my game development career that I felt both the weight of a large team relying on my work and the support of a large team working on a singular project together.

My primary responsibilities on this project were the core character controls and the game's tutorial systems. I was also responsible for hooking up all the character animations created by the team's artists. The character controls were built on a state machine with seven states: idle, walking, sprinting, sliding, jumping, airborne, and mantling. The key challenges I had to solve were in handling transitions between those states, both in terms of the physical forces acting on the character and the animation state changes. Working on this project sharpened my skills with Rigidbodies, collision logic, and general physics in a 3D gameplay environment.

Oxygen Caliber
Physics-based planetary puzzle platformer

Oxygen Caliber was a student project created for an intermediate game development course at USC. This project was the first where I really got my hands dirty with Unity and learned much of what I know about Unity's physics systems. In this course, we began prototyping by trying to answer a question about a gameplay experience. My partner and I chose to explore the question "Can a player successfully navigate a level using only recoil to move?" We liked the trajectory of the prototype, so we leaned into the physics mechanics. We put the character in space, built the game around planets with gravity fields, and added a grappling hook mechanic. Our vision was to create a movement system that felt smooth and easy to use but with a high skill ceiling and a large degree of freedom to give players room for creativity in their playstyle. The development of this game played a large part in solidifying my love of unique movement systems.
Bazooka Caverns
Explosive physics-based block-breaker game

I designed and developed Bazooka Caverns in 72 hours along with one other developer for Itch.io's Mini Jam 109: Depth(s). Itch.io's game jams always have a theme and a limitation; for this one, the theme was 'Depths' and the limitation was 'Health as a resource'. We created this physics-based brick-breaker puzzle game and placed top 11% among 152 entries. In Bazooka Caverns, the player's goal is to shoot bombs, break through blocks, and destroy a scorpion nest at the bottom of each level. The player can choose to spend health to increase the size and explosion radius of bombs, creating a dimension of strategy when planning how to best clear a level. My primary responsibilities on this project included game physics, collision logic, game state management, and audio implementation.
Par-a-Dice
Chaotic dice-rolling minigolf game

Par-a-dice was created by myself and two other developers in 48 hours for Itch.io's GMTK Game Jam 2022. The theme that year was 'Roll of the dice'. My partners and I wanted our game to incorporate the physical random roll of a die. We settled on a minigolf game in which the ball is a D20, and after each hit the resulting value on the die determines the power of the next hit. I was responsible for the D20-ball's physics, the player controls, and audio implementation. The biggest challenge I faced was in dealing with the realistic rolling physics of a D20. When a D20 is smacked around with high velocity, its roll is very chaotic and irregular. We chose to circumvent this by treating the ball as a sphere until it reaches a low enough speed and then swapping its collider for a realistic D20's. This way, the die's random rolling is meaningfully utilized, but the outcomes of a player's actions are still predictable enough for them to make choices.
UpWord
Unconventional word-building platformer

In an introductory mobile game development course at USC, I and eleven other developers created UpWord over one summer. Our goal was to mix together two of our favorite genres, word games and platformers. We dealt with some difficult design challenges on this project. We had trouble getting players to understand the objective of the game and the mechanics of submitting and clearing words. We struggled to hit the right blend of randomized letters and intentionally-sequenced letters so the player can actually spell words but still has a choice in what letters to collect. We also had issues with the balance of roles among the team; we had 11 programmers, 1 artist/programmer, and 0 dedicated designers, producers, audio engineers, etc. This meant all of us ended up being partial designers, and the collective image of the final product became muddy and unclear. My contributions to this project included word verification logic, game physics, audio implementation, and team management.
